## Core themes

This source is Coach Leo's most complete systems-style jungle guide. The value for the Don Sosa Coach knowledge base is not in any single rule, but in how the rules fit together:

1. Mindset should protect repeatable high-quality reps
2. Pregame should simplify the first decisions
3. Early game should run on tracking, tempo, and high-percentage actions
4. Mid game should be read through uptime, downtime, and pressure
5. Late game should close through objective identity, side pressure, and Baron structure

## Cleaned notes by concept

### 1. Climbing is a volume and quality problem

Leo repeats that rank stabilizes with sample size. This makes mentality practical:

- stop overreacting to tiny samples
- stop chasing fresh accounts
- protect the quality of your reps

The strongest reusable mental tools here are:

- give goals a 3-6 month window
- use at most two accounts
- prevent tilt sessions from turning neutral games into extra losses

### 2. Champion mastery reduces CPU load

The champion pool should stay small enough that the champion becomes background processing. If the player is using most of their mental stack on basic champion execution, they cannot track, path, or adapt well enough.

This is one of the cleanest justifications for a narrow pool:

- not only consistency
- also freed decision-making capacity

### 3. Champ select should create a first map idea

Leo wants champ select to answer:

- what does my champion identity want here
- what boots or build direction are likely
- where can I realistically get an early kill

The best reusable pregame framework from the video is VGM:

- volatility
- gank setup
- enemy mobility

Use VGM lane by lane to decide where the early game has real kill potential.

### 4. Early tracking starts before camps

The guide strongly emphasizes level-one wards and using them to infer pathing. One of the best reusable jungle concepts here is camp mirroring:

- if my camp 3 matches his camp 3
- my camp respawns inform his respawns
- his wasted time creates punish windows on camps I can predict

This is a very teachable bridge between tracking and concrete punish.

### 5. Information should be gathered with prompts

Leo does not frame camera movement as random checking. He wants the player to react to prompts:

- icons clashing
- health bars dropping
- lane state shifting
- enemy reveals

The goal is to gather information with intention and then make a plan before the decision window closes.

### 6. Weak side and strong side camps create map structure

After the first clear, players need to understand which camps belong to the side they want to spend time on. Clear weak side first so strong side remains the part of the map where you still have options:

- gank
- objective
- invade
- pressure

This is one of the strongest structure concepts in the guide.

### 7. Tempo is regular time and spare time

Leo divides jungle time into:

- regular tempo: camps are alive, so your action must be worth the cost
- spare tempo: dead time before camps or after a sequence, where you can ward, hover, check lanes, or help a wave

This is the operational version of "farm first, react to information."

### 8. Turn-based tempo prevents greed sequences

A very distinct concept in this guide is turn-based tempo:

- if the enemy jungler gets one move, you often only get one clean move before the board updates

This prevents the common error of trying to:

- counter jungle
- then dragon
- then gank

all inside a window that only supports one answer.

### 9. Ganks should be judged by wave, setup, body language, and timing

The video gives a strong high-percentage gank framework:

- choose the right wave
- arrive when the enemy is still committed to hitting the wave
- path to cut off the retreat
- read enemy body language and lane habits

This makes ganking more like timing and geometry, not only "lane looks pushed."

### 10. Match, trade, or minimize should govern objective play

This framework appears across the full guide:

- match when stronger
- trade when weaker
- minimize when neither match nor trade is realistic

Objectives, skirmishes, and mid-game turns all become clearer through this lens.

### 11. Dragon and grubs do not demand the same commitment

Leo explains dragon as a one-bar commitment and grubs as a split-bar objective. That difference matters because:

- dragon demands more certainty and more time
- grubs allow partial commitment and easier disengage

This is a strong concept for correcting bad early objective habits.

### 12. Greed and base timing must be read through map context

Leo is explicit that sitting on gold is not automatically bad. If:

- no objective is spawning
- key cooldowns are down
- clear is efficient
- no immediate contest matters

then greed can be correct. On the other hand, if the next objective is decisive and your buy changes fight strength, base for tempo.

### 13. Mid game is mostly uptime and downtime

Leo simplifies mid game around a few questions:

- is my team synced enough to pressure
- are tempo lines aligned
- what objective or tower are we playing for

When the answer is no, it is usually downtime:

- farm
- reset
- protect waves
- avoid forcing

### 14. Side lane shadowing converts pressure into picks

A strong mid-game lesson is to shadow the side lane that is creating pressure instead of grouping too literally. This lets the jungler:

- catch enemy responses
- protect the pusher
- rotate first when someone answers

### 15. Teamfights should follow champion identity

Leo's own Viego example is used to teach that a teamfight plan should come from the champion's identity. In his case:

- front to back
- wait for reset target
- do not dive randomly just because a fight started

This is a very reusable coaching point because many teamfight mistakes are identity mistakes first.

### 16. Baron closes games through waves, not only through grouping

The late-game closeout lesson is simple:

- with Baron, push all lanes
- force impossible responses
- rotate when enemies answer

The mistake is grouping too early and failing to use Baron to stretch the map.

## Most reusable coaching ideas for Don Sosa Coach

These are the strongest concepts to convert into cards:

- VGM: volatility, gank setup, enemy mobility
- camp mirroring to punish off-tempo enemy camps
- turn-based tempo: one move for one move
- choose greed or base according to the next objective window
- accept small sample variance and protect rep quality
- side lane shadowing in mid game
- teamfight through champion identity
- Baron closes through wave pressure, not only five-man grouping
