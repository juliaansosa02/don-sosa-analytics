# Coach Leo - Give Me 40 Minutes To Make You INSANE At Jungle

Source:
- Coach: Coach Leo
- Video: Give Me 40 Minutes To Make You INSANE At Jungle - Diamond Jungle Coaching
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3jOURGjldOM

## Core themes

This review is centered around one primary idea: a jungler's real strength is not only mechanics or good ganks, but how well they use time.

Coach Leo keeps returning to a few recurring concepts:

1. Enemy jungle awareness
2. Tempo and time usage
3. Camps up vs spare time
4. Match, trade, or minimize
5. Playing with certainty instead of gambling
6. Clear communication through pings
7. Positioning between lanes in mid game
8. Asking "what's next?" immediately after mistakes

## Cleaned notes by concept

### 1. Think about what the enemy jungler can do

The reviewed player was too focused on their own route and not focused enough on the enemy jungler's real options. The game plan should always include:

- which lane is volatile
- where the enemy jungler wants to path
- whether invade or early pressure is possible

The point is not memorizing every matchup in the game. The point is reading volatility through:

- champion tools
- CC
- damage patterns
- summoner spells
- keystones and runes

If bot lane is a volatile lane, then the enemy jungler's early route should be read through that lane.

### 2. Default timeline: farm, then react to information

Coach Leo frames jungle as a timeline:

- default state is farming
- new information creates a decision point
- then you return to default farming unless there is a high-value reason not to

This makes consistency much easier. A lot of bad jungle games come from replacing that timeline with low-confidence gambles.

### 3. Camps up time vs spare time

One of the biggest ideas in the review is the distinction between:

- **camp up time**: your camps are spawning or available, so your default should be tempo and farming
- **spare time**: you are waiting for camps or there is true dead time, so you can use it for vision, wards, tracking, wave help, or checking enemy camps

Players often waste spare time by doing nothing, but they also often ruin camp up time by forcing weak plays. Both are tempo leaks.

### 4. Use spare time productively

When no high-value play is guaranteed and camps are not yet spawning, spare time should be used for "spare time jobs":

- ward enemy camps
- remove blast cone
- sweep vision
- check a lane state
- help fix a wave if it is truly worth more than the alternative

The main lesson is that spare time should create information or setup, not idleness.

### 5. Punish enemy jungle plays when they go off-tempo

If the enemy jungler makes a top play and therefore cannot be on time for their next camp cycle, you should be thinking about what camp can be punished.

The player in the review missed windows where the enemy's raptors should have been taken because the enemy jungle had already shown top side.

This is a major difference between average and elite jungle play: recognizing when an enemy made a play but gave up tempo elsewhere.

### 6. Do not run to strong side without a guaranteed payoff

Coach Leo repeatedly points out that going directly to a strong-side play only makes sense if the player can say with confidence:

- I get dragon
- I kill the enemy jungler
- I kill bot lane

If none of those are certain, then the player is often just walking into downtime with nothing guaranteed while camps stay up elsewhere.

### 7. Ask "what's next?" after mistakes

After a death or bad play, the reviewed player kept mentally dwelling on the mistake. Coach Leo wants the next thought to be:

- what summoners were used
- what objective is next
- what path is next
- which lane or side becomes the next high-value target

The lesson is not to analyze in real time. Real-time play should move forward.

### 8. Match when stronger, trade when weaker, minimize when behind

If the enemy team commits numbers to Herald or another side and your team is down players, the answer is not to force a losing match.

Instead:

- if you are stronger, match
- if you are weaker, trade
- if nothing can be traded, minimize and protect your own map state

This is one of the cleanest mid game concepts in the session.

### 9. Position between two lanes in mid game

In mid game, the jungler should often be in the jungle space between the lanes their team is actively playing around. This lets them:

- take camps
- clear vision
- react to fights in either lane
- create picks

What the player kept doing instead was standing on towers, taking waves, or grouping too literally, which reduced their ability to threaten or react.

### 10. Cut off, do not only path to save

When rotating to a fight, Coach Leo emphasizes pathing to kill or cut off, not only pathing to "save" a teammate. The better angle is often the angle that traps the enemy escape instead of the angle that arrives closest to the teammate first.

### 11. Use pings to tell the team what the game means

Coach Leo distinguishes between weak pings and informative pings. Effective jungle communication should:

- identify the enemy jungler
- warn the lane before the play
- call the objective or assist play with intent

Jungle is described as "the motor of the game," which means the jungler should communicate the likely next move, not assume laners will infer it.

## Most reusable coaching ideas for Don Sosa Coach

These are the best concepts to turn into knowledge cards:

- enemy jungle options by volatile lane
- camps up time vs spare time
- punish off-tempo enemy camps
- only run strong side with guaranteed payoff
- what to do after a mistake: ask what's next
- trade instead of matching when behind numbers
- position between two lanes in mid game
- cut off instead of pathing only to save
- ping intention clearly as jungler
