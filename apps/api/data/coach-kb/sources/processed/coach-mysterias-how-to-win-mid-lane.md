## Cleaned notes by concept

### 1. Draft around team identity

The transcript frames mid lane draft as more than just "what beats the enemy lane." The better question is:

- what does my own composition need from mid?

If the team needs primary engage, pick accordingly. If the team already has dive and needs range or control, pick accordingly.

### 2. Offensive defense in lane

A strong lane concept in the source is that sometimes the safest lane state is the aggressive one. Against poke or long-range control, pushing first can reduce the enemy's ability to freely cast on you and gives you more control over spacing, wards, and movement.

### 3. Use push to gather information

Push is not just for CS or plates. Push allows the mid laner to:

- ward raptors or one side entrance
- move toward scuttle or river
- support jungle first
- deny enemy freedom

### 4. Play the side of the next useful action

Especially without teleport, the mid laner should not drift onto the weak side of the map while the important neutral objective or strongest teammate is on the other side. Good mid macro means placing yourself where the next real play is likely to happen.

### 5. Pick the best play, not just any play

One of the strongest phrases in the source is that the goal is not to take every possible action. The goal is to identify the best available play. That requires:

- reading strong side and weak side
- reading neutral objective states
- reading who is currently strong

### 6. Side lane to force a response

The mid-game side lane sequence is useful. The point of side lane pressure is not always to instantly dive or all-in. It is often to:

- force a predictable responder
- pin a weaker target
- earn first move into the next skirmish

### 7. Respect cooldown-gated identity

The Ahri examples are very transferable to other cooldown-gated playmakers. If your champion is heavily defined by one major cooldown, you should not take fights as if that cooldown is available when it is not.

### 8. Review awkward moments, not the entire VOD first

The post-game review method is strong:

- go to the moments that felt awkward
- identify the actual information gap or tempo error
- refine the decision that would have made the sequence cleaner

That is a fast-track review tactic because it connects live discomfort to a concrete lesson.
