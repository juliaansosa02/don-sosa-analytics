## SoloQ versus coordinated team play

This source is strongly SoloQ-oriented.

That matters because some advice changes when five players are coordinated:

- In SoloQ, you often assume people will die, misplay, or ignore ideal calls.
- In coordinated team play, you can invest more into setup-heavy group plays because communication raises the floor.
- In SoloQ, preserving your own agency is more important because fights often break before perfect setup exists.
- In coordinated play, more value comes from synchronizing waves, vision, and timings around shared calls.

For Don Sosa Coach, these ideas should usually be tagged or treated as:

- `soloq`
- `chaos management`
- `agency preservation`

and not blindly generalized to organized team environments.

## Core themes worth keeping

1. Spend your energy only on decisions you control
2. Do not compensate for every teammate mistake
3. You do not need to win every game to climb
4. Stay strong enough to punish the enemy's mistakes
5. Use click-and-pan to react before overanalyzing
6. Match, trade, or minimize based on strength and numbers
7. Uptime and downtime still exist when the game is chaotic
8. When behind or when your team is unstable, take small wins

## Cleaned notes by concept

### 1. Be selfish with your energy

The most reusable mental point is not "never feel frustrated." It is:

- do not spend energy typing
- do not spend energy blaming
- do not spend energy obsessing over LP or the outcome of one game

That energy should stay available for decisions, tracking, and adaptation.

### 2. Every game still contains decisions worth learning from

The transcript argues that even ugly games still contain one or two useful decisions to review. The practical takeaway is:

- do not rewrite your entire identity because of one bad game
- do not replace your system with panic adaptations
- extract one relevant improvement and move on

### 3. Do not compensate automatically

This is one of the strongest gameplay ideas in the source.

When a teammate dies or loses lane, the jungler should not automatically rush to "fix" it. Compensation often costs your own camps, route, tempo, or item timing and may still fail.

Better approach:

- judge if the play is real
- if not, keep tempo and preserve your own strength

### 4. Click and pan

Coach Leo describes a very useful micro-routine:

- click first so your champion is already moving
- then pan camera to analyze the situation

This reduces slow reactions and helps the player gather information while already moving toward the possible play.

### 5. Path to kill, not only to save

The source repeats a useful SoloQ carry principle:

- do not rotate only to rescue a teammate
- rotate with an angle that kills the enemy if they overextend

This preserves your ability to become the strong side of the game instead of being dragged into low-value rescue attempts.

### 6. Creativity has value, but it must stay low-risk relative to the reward

Some opportunities are not pure fundamentals like "just full clear." Sometimes there is a creative trap, posture, or patience angle that can create a kill. The useful distinction is:

- creative plays are good when the risk is low and the reward is high
- creativity is bad when it destroys your route for a speculative save

### 7. Match, trade, minimize

This source reinforces a very important framework:

- match when you and your team are strong enough
- trade when you cannot match
- minimize when you cannot trade

The key addition in this video is that this rule matters even more in SoloQ, where your teammates may die before ideal setup ever appears.

### 8. Preserve agency to punish enemy mistakes

The strongest carry principle in the transcript is this:

- the enemy team will also make mistakes
- you need to stay strong enough to punish them when they do

This is why overcompensating, overfighting, or throwing bounty on low-value contests is so costly. If you lose your own agency, enemy mistakes stop mattering.

### 9. Take small wins when the game is unstable

When the map is messy, the goal is not always to instantly swing the whole game. Often the correct move is:

- one kill
- one flash
- one objective
- one safe camp trade
- one wave protected

Small wins rebuild game state more reliably than desperate all-ins.

### 10. Uptime and downtime still apply in losing games

The source explains that even in bad or chaotic games:

- when your team is synced and able to pressure, you can play uptime
- when teammates are dead, basing, or scattered, it is usually downtime

During downtime, you should often:

- farm
- base
- avoid throw zones
- wait for a better turn
