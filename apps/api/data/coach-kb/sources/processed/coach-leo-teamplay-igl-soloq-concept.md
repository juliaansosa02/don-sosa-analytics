## Core themes

This source is centered around a narrower but very powerful idea: team play in SoloQ is not blind obedience. Good team play means creating cleaner map decisions than the lobby average and helping your team sync around those decisions.

Coach Leo keeps returning to these recurring concepts:

1. Be an in-game leader instead of only a follower
2. Ping the player and the play, not just your emotion
3. Sync pressure with your team's actual tempo lines
4. Accept reality when a play breaks instead of chain inting
5. Let teammates make their own play when your arrival is too late
6. Use short communication to stabilize mental
7. Mute to preserve accountability, not to avoid learning

## Cleaned notes by concept

### 1. Team play is not blind following

Coach Leo's main correction is that players often confuse "team play" with "doing what teammates are already doing." That is not real team play in SoloQ.

Real team play means:

- reading numbers and pressure correctly
- making your own call
- helping the team play around the best available map idea

If you only follow the average lobby call, your macro ceiling often stays trapped near the level of the players you are following.

### 2. Every role can be an IGL

The jungler has the easiest access to the whole map, but Leo is explicit that every role can short call:

- top can ping numbers and TP windows
- mid can ping enemy movement and objective turns
- support can lead setup and vision
- bot lane can control objective arrivals

The reusable point is that better climbing requires some level of assertive communication, not only reactive play.

### 3. Ping who you want and what you want

Leo's most concrete communication point is the two-part ping:

- ping the teammate icon
- ping the objective or location

Examples:

- ping bot lane, then ping scuttle
- ping mid lane, then ping danger on the enemy jungle path
- ping top lane, then ping dragon or invade

The value is precision. One or two purposeful pings often outperform vague spam.

### 4. Sync pressure with your team's tempo

Mid game pressure only works when your team is actually on the map. If your team is dead, basing, late, or scattered, forcing an invade or objective often creates chain inting.

The important rule is:

- pressure when your team can use the pressure
- downtime when your team has casualties or broken tempo lines

### 5. Break the chain instead of compensating

One of the strongest reusable ideas in the video is that players often think team play means joining a bad chain because teammates already committed.

Leo's correction:

- if your support gets caught, do not automatically continue uptime
- if top and support die, that does not force you to die too
- preserve the map by farming, resetting, protecting waves, or waiting for the next synced round

This is a direct anti-chain-int principle.

### 6. Accept reality fast

If the fight went badly, the next job is not to argue with the game state. The next job is to accept the current map and play the next round correctly.

This means:

- stop insisting on the same objective if numbers changed
- stop forcing because the original plan "should" have worked
- play the hand that the map now gives you

### 7. Let your team cook when you cannot arrive in time

Not every ally play requires full participation. If a side play starts while you are on camps and your arrival would be late, the correct move can be:

- keep farming efficiently
- take nearby wave or camp value
- collect information from enemy movement
- convert on the opposite side after they show

This is still team play because you are using the pressure the play creates, even without joining it directly.

### 8. Small language can stabilize mental

Leo softens his older "just mute all" stance by pointing out that short phrases like "my bad" can reduce blame spirals and keep the game playable.

For the knowledge base, the useful takeaway is not "type more." It is:

- short, low-ego communication can preserve game state
- long chat plans usually do not

### 9. Mute for accountability and emotional control

Leo still defends muting when needed, but frames it more carefully:

- mute when you know pings or chat will influence you emotionally
- keep pings if they still provide useful information
- mute all can be a tool for accountability because it removes the excuse of "my team pinged me into it"
